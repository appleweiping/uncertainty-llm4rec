# Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used in recommendation systems, especially in generative recommendation settings where a model is asked to produce, explain, or reorder items conditioned on a user's interaction history. This creates a reliability problem that is sharper than in many open-ended language tasks: recommendation outputs must correspond to catalog items, and in many practical pipelines they must be comparable within a retrieved or sampled candidate set. Prior LLM-as-ranker work often frames this problem as conditional ranking over retrieved candidates, where the output space is constrained by the candidate list. Generative recommendation, by contrast, raises additional item-grounding and calibration challenges because a fluent generated title may not correspond to a valid catalog item, and a plausible explanation or confidence score does not by itself establish that the recommendation is correct.

The central difficulty is that a generative recommender can output a recommendation that is fluent, plausible, and high confidence, while still being invalid, ungrounded, outside the candidate set, or simply wrong for the held-out next item. This failure mode is not just a parsing nuisance: if the generated item is promoted in a ranking pipeline, the system may replace a reasonable fallback decision with an unsupported intervention. We therefore study the following question: "Can LLM uncertainty be transformed from an unreliable self-report into a calibrated decision signal for ranking?"

Our empirical motivation comes from strict catalog-grounded experiments in this repository's MovieLens candidate-500 protocol. Under that protocol, free-form generated-title recommendation underperformed or failed as ranking evidence, and verbalized confidence was highly miscalibrated. High-confidence wrong outputs were common in the retained MovieLens R3 artifacts. Moreover, conservative uncertainty gating avoided harm only by degenerating to the fallback ranker, rather than reliably identifying useful LLM interventions. These observations do not imply that all LLM recommendation is ineffective; instead, they suggest that open-ended generation and direct self-reported confidence are the wrong interface for using LLMs in a grounded ranking problem.

We propose CU-GR v2, Candidate-Normalized Calibrated Preference Fusion for Generative Recommendation. Rather than trusting free-form title generation or verbalized confidence as a direct decision, CU-GR v2 constrains the LLM to a candidate-local panel of valid items and asks for listwise preference scores over anonymous item labels. The resulting scores are parsed, normalized within the candidate panel, and fused with a fallback ranker under validation-selected safety constraints. The method treats the LLM as a source of local preference information, not as an unconstrained generator of final catalog decisions.

CU-GR v2 builds each candidate panel from fallback ranks, popularity and tail contrasts, sequential signals when available, and candidate-adherent LLM proposals when such artifacts exist. The prompt presents anonymous candidate labels together with user history context and item titles or metadata, and requires a structured JSON ranking. The parser maps labels back to item identifiers, rejects unknown or duplicate labels, and preserves raw outputs for audit. A calibrated fusion policy combines normalized fallback score, normalized LLM listwise score, LLM confidence, and a popularity penalty. The final ranking only reorders valid candidate items; non-panel candidates preserve their fallback order.

Under our sampled-candidate protocol, CU-GR v2 improves held-out seed42 NDCG@10 over fallback ranking on two datasets. On MovieLens 1M with candidate_size=500 and panel_size=15, fallback NDCG@10 is 0.049339 and CU-GR v2 reaches 0.106060, a delta of +0.056721. On Amazon Beauty, where the local catalog has 479 items and candidate_size is therefore 479, fallback NDCG@10 is 0.142486 and CU-GR v2 reaches 0.154217, a delta of +0.011731. These results use subset_size=200 LLM-scored examples per seed and should be interpreted as sampled-candidate evidence, not full-ranking, production-scale, or more-than-two-domain validation. Harmful swaps are controlled but not eliminated: the held-out harmful swap rates are 0.035 on MovieLens and 0.015 on Amazon Beauty.

Our contributions are:

1. We formulate uncertainty-aware generative recommendation as a grounded candidate-ranking problem rather than free-form title generation.
2. We provide an empirical analysis showing that free-form generation and verbalized confidence are unreliable under strict catalog-grounded evaluation in the retained MovieLens artifacts.
3. We propose CU-GR v2, a candidate-normalized calibrated preference fusion method for using LLM listwise signals inside a fallback ranking pipeline.
4. We validate CU-GR v2 on MovieLens 1M and Amazon Beauty with real DeepSeek v4 flash calls, saved raw outputs, cost and latency records, and reproducible paper artifacts.
5. We provide failure analyses, harmful swap analysis, and explicit limitations for sampled-candidate LLM recommendation.
